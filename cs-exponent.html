Designing Restraint into an AI Interface

In one line

I was brought on to prove that users couldn't understand a pre-launch AI wearable. The research showed the opposite — they understood it fine; they didn't feel in control of it. I reframed the problem, translated a vague feeling into a concrete interaction spec, and turned that spec into a system-level fix that held across every screen instead of the few we tested.

Results

  • The dominant complaint — cognitive overwhelm — stopped surfacing as a primary theme when the same usability protocol was re-run after the overhaul (the explicit goal the work was scoped against)
  • The fix shipped at the token and component level, so restraint became the system default rather than a per-screen patch that would regrow on the next screen
  • Research-to-design latency dropped from a ~2-week synthesis hand-off to insight-to-iteration inside a single sprint
  • Pain-point maps were adopted directly into the design team's working files — pulled into Figma annotations rather than filed in a deck
  • Promoted from research intern to contract product designer on the strength of the contribution

The Reframe

The brief I inherited carried an assumption: users were struggling with feature comprehension — too much to learn, too many things to figure out. The implied fix was education — tooltips, onboarding, progressive teaching.

That's not what the sessions showed. Participants could explain what features did. They could complete tasks. What they couldn't do was predict the interface. AI-driven elements updated on their own, surfaced proactively, and changed multiple regions of the screen at once. The language was strikingly consistent across participants: overwhelmed, distracted, like it's doing too much, like I'm not driving.

That's not a comprehension problem. It's a control problem — and the two have opposite fixes. Teaching someone more about a system that already feels out of their hands makes the feeling worse, not better. Getting that distinction right before the team committed engineering time was the highest-leverage thing I did on the project.

So I had to defend the reframe, not just assert it. "Users feel overwhelmed" is a sentiment, and a sentiment isn't a design input. My job was to make it falsifiable and specific enough that design and engineering could build against it.

Research Process

Facilitating for behavior, not for agreement

I ran usability sessions across multiple participant cohorts with one discipline above all: don't rescue the participant, and don't confirm the team's prior. I let people struggle, stayed quiet through the awkward pauses, and captured behavioral observations — hesitations, backtracks, the moment a hand lifted off the device — timestamped to the specific task step rather than logged as loose impressions.

Auditing the data before trusting it

Before any of it could drive a decision, I audited the session logs and participant records and found inconsistencies that would have poisoned anything built on top of them. I treated it as a design problem — bad inputs produce confident, wrong outputs — so I cleaned the data, flagged the gaps explicitly, and only then let the findings move forward. Reframing a project on the back of dirty data would have been worse than not reframing it at all.

Synthesis that exposes pattern, not anecdote

I clustered behavioral observations, verbatim quotes, and survey responses by theme rather than by participant using affinity mapping. Organizing by theme is what turned "three people mentioned this" into "this is a structural pattern at a specific interaction point" — the difference between an anecdote a PM can wave away and a severity rating a team has to schedule against. The output was annotated flows marking exact failure moments, pain-point maps with severity ratings, and reports built to be scannable in a 30-second design review and deep-readable when someone needed the evidence.

From Sentiment to Spec

This was the core analytical work, and the part I'm proudest of. I decomposed "overwhelmed" into the observable interface behaviors that were producing it. Across the annotated sessions, the perception of lost control consistently traced back to three mechanisms:

  • Simultaneity — multiple regions of the screen updated at the same time, so there was no single place to look and no stable anchor to return to.
  • Unpredictability — the interface acted without a user-initiated trigger, and gave no signal before it acted, so changes felt like they came from nowhere.
  • Irreversibility — once a proactive behavior fired, there was no obvious way to dismiss it or get back to the prior state, so users couldn't recover from a surprise even after they understood it.

Framed that way, the felt experience of "I'm not in control" becomes a function of simultaneity × unpredictability × irreversibility — and every one of those is a design lever, not a personality of the AI. That decomposition is what made the problem buildable. Instead of handing the team a feeling, I handed them three specific failure modes mapped to the exact UI moments where each one fired.

The Design Response

Each mechanism mapped to a deliberate design move, built as system rules rather than one-off screen fixes:

Against simultaneity: one thing moves at a time

AI-initiated updates were sequenced into a single active focus zone and choreographed so only one region animated at once. Anything not relevant to the user's current context held still and stepped back in the visual hierarchy. The interface had to earn attention rather than demand it everywhere by default.

Against unpredictability: telegraph before acting

Proactive behaviors got a brief, legible pre-state — a signal that something was about to happen and why it was surfacing — instead of appearing fully formed. Predictability is what converts a surprise into an expectation, and an expectation doesn't read as a loss of control.

Against irreversibility: every proactive action is interruptible

If the system could act on its own, the user had to be able to see it, stop it, and undo it. Reversibility was treated as a precondition for proactivity, not a nice-to-have bolted on afterward.

Why it shipped as a system, not a set of screens

All of this landed at the token and component level: a defined notion of "proactivity level," distinct component states for AI-initiated vs. user-initiated changes, and motion rules baked into the system. Had I patched only the screens we happened to test, the overwhelm would have regrown on every new screen the team built after I left. Encoding restraint as the default behavior of the components is what made the fix durable.

The Hard Call

There was real pressure — from the product side and from the calendar — to do the opposite of restraint. A pre-launch AI product wants its intelligence to be visible: more proactive surfacing, more "look what it can do," because that's what demos well to stakeholders and reviewers. And with launch close, the fast path was to patch the specific screens we'd tested and call it done.

I argued against both. The behavior that made the product impressive in a 90-second demo was the same behavior that was costing trust in daily use — proactivity that reads as magic on first contact reads as noise by day three. And a screen-level patch under deadline would have looked like progress while leaving the actual mechanism intact. I pushed for fewer, quieter, more legible AI behaviors implemented as system rules, even though it was the slower and less flashy path. Restraint is a harder sell than capability, but it was the call the research supported.

Closing the Loop

Past the product itself, I found the research-to-design hand-off was where insight went to die. Studies were finishing on time, but findings reached designers slowly — a two-week latency between a session ending and its insight becoming a design input, by which point the sprint had moved on.

I partnered with research and engineering to restructure how session data was captured, cleaned, and reported, so a finding could become a tested design response inside the same sprint instead of arriving as a synthesis doc a fortnight late. Impact from research isn't automatic — it's earned by making findings usable. A pain-point map that lives in Notion gets read once; one embedded in the designer's working file becomes part of how they think. That's why the team started pulling verbatim quotes straight into Figma annotations.

How I knew it worked

The measure was deliberately the same instrument as the diagnosis: re-run the original usability protocol after the overhaul and watch whether cognitive overwhelm still surfaced as a primary theme. It didn't. I'm candid that this is a qualitative bar rather than a single hero metric — given the room to redo it, I'd have added a per-task perceived-control rating to put a number on the shift, which is exactly the gap I'd close next time.

Results

  • Cognitive overwhelm no longer surfaced as a primary complaint when the original usability protocol was re-run post-overhaul — the explicit goal the work was scoped against
  • Restraint shipped as a system default at the token/component level, so the fix held across screens built after the engagement, not just the ones tested
  • Research-to-design latency cut from a ~2-week synthesis hand-off to insight-to-iteration within a single sprint
  • Pain-point maps adopted into the design team's working files and used for sprint prioritization
  • Promoted from research intern to contract product designer based on the scope of the contribution

Reflection

What I'd do differently

I'd instrument the outcome quantitatively from the start — a perceived-control or task-confidence rating captured every session — so the before/after was a number, not a disappearance of a theme. The reframe was right, but I made the case largely on qualitative evidence, and a clean metric would have made it bulletproof and easier to defend upward.

What I took from it

AI interfaces break an assumption most UI patterns are built on: the interface isn't only responding to the user, it's anticipating them — and anticipation that the user can't predict or stop reads as lost control, no matter how smart it is. Restraint has to be the default, and proactivity has to be earned, telegraphed, and reversible. The instinct that carried the project is the one I bring to every system: name the mechanism, then fix it at the component level — or you'll keep fixing the same problem on every screen forever.
